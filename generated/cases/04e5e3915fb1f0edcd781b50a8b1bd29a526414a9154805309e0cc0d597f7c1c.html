Lab Exercise 3: Rmarkdown
Sources of Empathy in the Circuit Court
In this lab, we practice two skills at the same time: 1. Writing clean, reproducible analysis in R
Markdown 2. Creating clear visualizations with appropriate labels and interpretation. We will analyze
the relationship between the gender composition among a judge’s children and voting behavior among
circuit court judges. In a recent paper, Adam N. Glynn and Maya Sen argue that having a female child
causes circuit court judges to make more pro-feminist decisions. The paper can be found at:
• Glynn, Adam N., and Maya Sen. (2015). “Identifying Judicial Empathy: Does Having Daughters
Cause Judges to Rule for Women’s Issues?. ” American Journal of Political Science Vol. 59, No. 1,
pp. 37–54.
Download the data ( judges.csv)
from Canvas or webpage The dataset contains the following variables about individual judges:
Variable Description
num Entry number
name The judge’s name
circuit Wh ich federal circuit the judge serves in.
child Th e number of children each judge has.
girls The number of female children the judge has.
sons The nu mber of male children the judge has.
woman Take s a value of 1 if the judge is a woman, 0 otherwise.
yearb Th e year the judge was born.
race Th e judge’s race (1 = white, 2 = African-American, 3 = Hispanic, 4 =
Asian-American).
religion T he judge’s religion (1 = Unitarian, 2 = Episcopalian, 3 = Baptist, 4 =
Catholic, 5 = Jewish, 7 = Presbyterian, 8 = Protestant, 9 =
Congregationalist, 10 = Methodist, 11 = Church of Christ, 16 = Baha’i,
17 = Mormon, 21 = Anglican, 24 = Lutheran, 99 = unknown).
republican Takes a value of 1 if the judge was appointed by a Republican president,
0 otherwise. Used as a proxy for the judge’s party.
progressive.vote The proportion of the judge’s votes on women’s issues which were
decided in a pro-feminist direction.
1
(1) Report the number of observations and variables in the dataset using inline R code.
Then create a row-wise proportion table that shows the distribution of republican (Dem
appointee vs. Rep appointee) within woman (Man vs. W oman). Finally , display the table
with clean, readable labels (do not leave values coded as 0/1).
(2) Create a histogram of progressive.vote that includes a clear title and labeled axes.
Choose a reasonable number of bins (don’t rely on the default without thinking). Then
write 2–4 sentences describing what the histogram shows—where most values cluster,
whether the distribution looks skewed or roughly symmetric, and whether there is no-
ticeable bunching near 0 or near 1.
(3) Create a new 4-category variable called gender_party that combines woman and re-
publican into the following groups: Dem Man, Dem W oman, Rep Man, and Rep W oman.
Then compute the mean of progressive.vote within each group. Finally , make a bar chart
of these four group means with clear labels and a short title, and write 2–4 sentences
interpreting the main pattern you see.
(4) Restrict the dataset to judges who have at least one child (child > 0). Create an
indicator for whether a judge has at least one daughter (1 if girls >= 1 , 0 otherwise).
Then use side-by-side boxplots to compare the distribution of progressive.vote for judges
with no daughters versus judges with at least one daughter. Next, compute the difference in
mean progressive vote share between the two groups (at least one daughter − no daughters)
and report that value using inline R code. Finally , write 2–3 sentences explaining what
the boxplot reveals that a simple mean comparison might hide (for example, differences in
spread, skewness, or outliers).
