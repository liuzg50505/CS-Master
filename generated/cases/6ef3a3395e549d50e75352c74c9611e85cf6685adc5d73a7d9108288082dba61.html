Abstract
This report consists of two different statistical computing projects that were advanced methods to implement two
common Bayesian statistical analysis methodologies the bayesian inference method and the monte carlo integration
method. Project 12 used the importance sampling technique to numerically compute the integral of unbounded
integrands. Compared traditional monte carlo techniques to targeted sampling strategies. Project 19 applied what
is known as bayesian inference for binomial proportion to determine the likelihood of failure of a machine (using the
conjugate prior to do the analysis, performing posterior computations and producing predictive models).

4. Expected Results
Using the incomplete Gamma function to theoretically obtain the integral gives approximately an integral value of
1.494. The variance is high and the rate of convergence low when using uniform sampling due to the integrand
having a singularity at its origin. Conversely, importance sampling has a much greater convergence rate and variance
reduction due to sampling preferentially at points that yield greater values of the integrand. Therefore, importance
sampling has shown itself to be much more efficient than uniform sampling both at small and moderate sample sizes,
and it has illustrated the importance of the design of the proposal distribution for Monte Carlo integration.
Project 19
1. Introduction
Bayesian inference is a logical process of changing the way we view estimations from known or unknown or to
estimated parameters based on both a prior set of values and observations recorded from a previous point in time.
The Bayesian method provides a distribution (probability) for every parameter based on all possible values that
measured from previous dataset (obtaining as much information needed or reducing the amount of uncertainty
related to your estimates).
This project looks into answering the most fundamental questions about scheduling maintenance. In that respect,
given that you have an observation of future failure records for the same machine in a much larger number of “time”
intervals (for e.g.. multiple inspection periods), does our observation of current failures in the past help to estimate
what the current rate of the machine is going to be? The Bayesian mode of analysis has eliminated uncertainty
from each level of analysis (or any process) involved with estimating parameters (values) to predictive estimation of
parameters.
2. Methodology
2.1 Model Specification
Let X be the number of failings experienced on each of the i-th day, and let X have a binomial distribution with
unknown parameter p which is uniformly distributed over the interval [0, 1]. The value n represents the number of
independent observations made on the i-th day and is fixed. We will use a total of 10 daily trials each of which will
contain failure counts from 10 consecutive days’ periods.

2.2 Prior Distribution and Conjugate Analysis
Analytical posterior computation is made feasible by a Beta conjugate prior for the binomial likelihood, p ∼ Beta(a, b),
where the prior is specified as p follows the Beta distribution with a and b parameters both equal to 1. As this prior
is a non-informative prior, it provides little or no prior information and therefore allows the posterior inference to be
predominantly influenced by the observed data.
The prior density is:
π(p) = pa−1(1 − p)b−1
B(a, b) = 1, p ∈ [0, 1]
where B(·, ·) denotes the Beta function.

According to Bayes’ theorem, the following relation exists regarding the posterior density function:
π(p | x) ∝ L(p | x) × π(p) ∝ pa+∑ xi−1(1 − p)b+∑ ni−∑ xi−1

By substituting the values of the data into the above relation:
p | x ∼ Beta(1 + 24, 1 + 100 − 24) = Beta(25, 77)
The two shape parameters of the posterior Beta distribution (25, 77) can be interpreted as being comprised of the
information related to the corresponding uniform prior (1, 1) plus the information obtained through the data collection
process (24 successes, 76 failures). The bulk of the posterior probability mass is positioned at the lower end (close
to zero) consistent with the fact that only two successes were observed out of 100 attempts. .

4. Results and Discussion
4.1 Posterior Inference
The Beta (25, 77) posterior distribution concentrates about p ≈ 0.245 and the 95% credible interval is (0.165, 0.334).
Therefore, the credible interval provides an estimate of the uncertainty associated with the estimate of the failure
rate while excluding the probability of the failure rate being greater than 0.334 with 95% of the probability.
