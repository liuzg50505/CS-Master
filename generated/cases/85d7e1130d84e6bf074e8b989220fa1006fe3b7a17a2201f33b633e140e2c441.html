Errata
1. Introduction
In many subjects, and specifically in your graphics course, you often worked with vectors and matrices.
Vectors were an (ordered) array of numbers, mostly from the real numbers, and matrices were two-
dimensional arrays. You used vectors with 3 elements to encode (x, y, z) coordinates, and 3 × 3 matrices
to transform coordinates (for instance, rotate them). You also used 4 × 4 matrices for world-view
coordinate transformations, and perspective transformations. In these notes, we aim to give a more
formal background to what vectors and matrices are, and how transformations of this sort work. This is
by no means a full account; we refer the reader to some fundamental books, written with the helpful
context of computer graphics in the reading list on the course webpage.
2. Vector Spaces
2.1. Vectors and Scalars. We define the space Rd as “the space of arrays of d real numbers” .
Cd is similarly defined for complex numbers. These arrays are denoted as vectors. Every single number
in the underlying field R or C (as such, an element of the vector) is called a scalar. We define two basic
operations on vectors:
(1) Addition. For two vectors v1 and v2, v3 = v1 + v2 is another vector. Geometrically, it
is achieved by concatenating v1 and v2 head to tail (the order doesn’t matter! addition is
commutative).
(2) Multiplication with a scalar. For a scalar λ and a vector v, λv is another vector, which
geometrically is just uniformly scaled by λ.
You will have noticed that the two operations create new vectors from “old” ones. That means that
given some set of vectors, we can always create more. How should we quantify the set of all such created
vectors? For this we have the following definition:
Definition 2.1. A vector space is a set of vectors that is closed under multiplication with scalars
and vector addition.
Here “closed” means that every new vector created from old vectors in the space is also within the
space—you cannot create vectors outside the space from vectors inside the space using these operations.
Here are some prominent examples of vector spaces (check that you indeed have the required closure):

In fact, vectors and vector spaces are more abstract concepts than mere objects as subspaces of Rd.
Every object can be defined as a “vector” if it has a meaningful definition for addition and multiplication
with a scalar. As a nice example, take spheres in space: we define “addition“ as creating the sphere that
envelopes both tightly, and multiplication by scalar as inflating (or deflating) them by multiplying the
radius with the scalar. If a sphere is a “vector”, then the set of spheres with these operations is a vector
space. However, we will—mostly—limit the discussion in the course to “classical” vectors for purposes
of coherency.

vectors u and v, and outputs a scalar s. In Rd, the default inner product is the dot product operator

the dot product is a measure of similarity between two vectors. In Rd, we have that u · v = |u| |v| cos(α),
where |u| is the length of u (and resp. |v| for v), and α is the angle between them. From this, we can

• The length of a vector u is nothing but |u| = √u · u. That means that an inner product also
induces a norm |·| on the space, allowing us to measure vector lengths. Note that the squared
norm is what you are used to from graphics: |u|2 = u · u = (a1)2 + . . .(ad)2.
• Two vectors are orthogonal if and only if u · v = 0, which means the angle between them is π
2 .
• Two vectors are codirectional (point in the same direction) if and only if u · v = |u| |v|. If you
keep u fixed and start rotating v, you will always get a lower result for u · v; the lowest result is
when v would point exactly opposite to u. That is directly as cos(0) = 1 while cos(π) = −1.
The above corollaries show why the dot product is a measure of similarity: you can obtain the angle
between two vectors as cos(α) = u·v
|u||v|, where size and sign of the dot product shows how much u is
aligned with v.
In the special, yet important case of unit-length vectors, where |u| = |v| = 1, cos(α) = u · v, and
therefore ⟨u, v⟩ is always between [−1, 1].

We use a set of linearly independent vectors as a spanning set (“spanning” is the more formal term for
“creating”, although the latter is used in a similar context). That is, we can define a vector space that
comprises all the possible vectors spanned by this set. Denote this set as V = {v1, . . . , vk}, and then
the spanned vector space is span(V ). As such, intuitively, linearly-dependent vectors have a redundancy
in representation; that means that one or more of the vectors can be linearly spanned by other vectors in
the set, and therefore they don’t add information to the set.

linearly independent set due to minimality, and we denote the cardinality of the set (number of vectors)
as the dimension of the spanned vector space. Dimension is intuitively the “power of expression“ of a
basis. in Rd, one describes vectors as d independent numbers (coordinates), and therefore the dimension
