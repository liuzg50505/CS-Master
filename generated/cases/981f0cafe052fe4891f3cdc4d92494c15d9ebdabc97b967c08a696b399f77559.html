Frequently Asked Questions
How should I interpret Turnitin's AI writing percentage and false positives?
The percentage shown in the AI writing report is the amount of qualifying text within the submission that Turnitin’s AI writing
detection model determines was either likely AI-generated text from a large-language model or likely AI-generated text that was
likely revised using an AI paraphrase tool or word spinner.
False positives (incorrectly flagging human-written text as AI-generated) are a possibility in AI models.
AI detection scores under 20%, which we do not surface in new reports, have a higher likelihood of false positives. To reduce the
likelihood of misinterpretation, no score or highlights are attributed and are indicated with an asterisk in the report (*%).
The AI writing percentage should not be the sole basis to determine whether misconduct has occurred. The reviewer/instructor
should use the percentage as a means to start a formative conversation with their student and/or use it to examine the submitted
assignment in accordance with their school's policies.
What does 'qualifying text' mean?
Our model only processes qualifying text in the form of long-form writing. Long-form writing means individual sentences contained in paragraphs that make up a
longer piece of written work, such as an essay, a dissertation, or an article, etc. Qualifying text that has been determined to be likely AI-generated will be
highlighted in cyan in the submission, and likely AI-generated and then likely AI-paraphrased will be highlighted purple.
Non-qualifying text, such as bullet points, annotated bibliographies, etc., will not be processed and can create disparity between the submission highlights and the
percentage shown.

Abstract
Speech Emotion Recognition (SER) refers to the recognition of human emotions through
spoken communication. SER is crucial to Speech Processing research.. The
Comprehensive and Novel Workflow outlined in this document allows for the processing
and parsing of the audio signal data into representative emotional states. This is done
using the Wav2Vec2.0 Model for extracting features from the audio data in conjunction
with three separate algorithms for identifying the emotional states. To aid the
development of the workflow, the CREMA-D Dataset was obtained, comprising 7442
individual audio files for analysis and emotional labelling. The six emotions in CREMA-D
are; Anger, Disgust, Fear, Happiness, Neutral, and Sadness. By using the Wav2Vec2.0
Algorithm for extracting features, the current understanding of Speech Emotion
Recognition will continue to expand and improve. The LSTM (Long-Term/Short Term
Memory), KNN (K-Nearest Neighbours), and Random Forest classification models were
used to identify the emotional states represented in CREMA-D. The overall comparative
performance of the different algorithms used to create the classification models is shown
through the Macro F1 Score, Accuracy, and Area under the Macro F1 Score for each
classification model with respect to the emotional states represented in CREMA-D. This
study demonstrates the benefits of using deep learning methods within the field of
Speech Emotion Recognition. As such, this study will assist future research into Speech
Emotion Recognition, while also providing insight into future capabilities that will result
from utilizing pre-trained models and classification methods.
Keywords: Speech Emotion Recognition; Wav2Vec2.0; LSTM; KNN; Random Forest;
CREMA-D Data Set; Machine Learning.

Traditional Methods of Speech Emotion Recognition Used Manually Engineered Features.
Mel-frequency cepstral coefficients - MFCCs are used within the field of speech
processing as part of a number of different features which represent the properties of the
human voice. MFCCs also require many hours of training and experience in the
application area where one is attempting to classify speech, so it is impossible to create
effective MFCCs without a thorough understanding of the area where you will be
providing them. For instance, one will encounter problems when classification attempts
are made with background noise or variability among speakers. Also, the process of
manually creating features is tedious and requires much time and effort to complete.
The development of Deep Learning technology has greatly changed the way we view
speech recognition tasks. Pre-trained Deep Learning models, such as Wav2Vec2.0 have
shown recently that the ability to create deep learning-based models for speech
recognition has now moved to being able to extract features from raw audio files directly
from the file itself, rather than having to manually create features from the audio files [1, 2].
The creation of Deep learning-based models can also assist us in identifying complex
relationships in speech data that we would not have previously found with traditional
methods, such as with the use of MFCCs.
This particular study will seek to determine how successful Wav2Vec2.0 is at performing
emotion recognition on speech. In order to accomplish this goal, this study will compare
the use of three different classification approaches: LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory)
network; K-Nearest Neighbour (KNN); and Random Forest methods.
The training and evaluation of the classification methods used within this study will come
from the CREMA-D dataset, which has been collected, compiled, and made available to
researchers for many years, and provides researchers with an excellent collection of
diverse speech emotion recordings from numerous different actors.
There are multiple objectives of this study; first, the study will identify how Wav2Vec2.0
can be effectively used to extract features from speech files for Emotion Recognition
purposes; second, the study will compare the performance of the three classifications
used within this study (LSTM, KNN, and Random Forest methods); third, the strengths
and limitations of all approaches will be discussed; and fourth; the last objective of this
study is to provide practical information needed for anyone wishing to create a Speech
Emotion Recognition System.
The Wav2Vec2.0 model was used in the study based on the CREMA-D dataset and
English language with no adjustment to the pre-trained model. The approach for
classifying the focused features from CREMA-D will be based on the data, with the
features being extracted from their source. However, the overall approach to training and
developing the Wav2Vec2.0 extracted features will be the same across all languages and
datasets. The limitations of the study include: that the findings may not be applied to any
language or dataset other than English and that the compute resources required for
training Wav2Vec2.0 extracted features will be quite substantial. The research also does
not address the use of data augmentation or advanced modelling techniques.

2 Literature Review
This literature review will investigate previously published work related to speech
emotion recognition, and how the background context provides insight into this current
study. Speech emotion recognition has been extensively studied in recent years, with
comprehensive reviews highlighting the evolution from traditional methods to deep
learning approaches [5].
2.1 Speech Emotion Recognition
The field of Speech Emotion Recognition has been a topic of considerable interest for
many years. Traditionally, emotion was detected through the manual extraction of
acoustic features such as pitch, energy, and frequency; then using traditional machine
learning classifiers such as Support Vector Machines (SVM) and Gaussian Mixture
Models (GMM) for reasonable success under laboratory-controlled environments.
Unfortunately, many of these methods have experienced difficulties when applied in real
life because of various factors.
More recently, advances in Deep Learning techniques have greatly improved the Speech
Emotion Recognition field through the use of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) that
can identify features from spectrograms, and Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN) that
model the temporal dependencies of speech. These new techniques have produced
significantly better results compared to traditional techniques, but a considerable volume
of labelled training data is still needed for their success [4, 6].
The recent trend of using pre-trained models, such as Wav2Vec2.0 and HuBERT, to
develop speech recognition systems, has shown that significant advances can be made
in a very short period of time, without the need to create large datasets for training.
Fine-tuning these models for specific tasks can be done using low amounts of labeled
data. This strategy has proven successful across a wide variety of applications on the
speech processing side of things [1, 2].
2.2 Wav2Vec2.0
Wav2Vec2.0 is an example of a self-supervised learning model created by Facebook AI
Research. Wav2Vec 2.0 was released in 2020: The primary objective of this model is to
learn representations directly from raw audio. In order to do this, Wav2Vec2.0 uses
Contrastive Learning. The architecture contains two main segments: A feature encoder
and a Transformer network. The feature encoder takes raw audio inputs and creates
latent (hidden) representations from them to illustrate sound, while the Transformer
network utilizes surrounding timesteps (timeframes) to create the output.
The model learns to predict the masked sections of a song during pre-training. Doing so
