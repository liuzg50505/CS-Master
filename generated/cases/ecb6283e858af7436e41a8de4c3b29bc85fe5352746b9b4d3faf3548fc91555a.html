2 Introduction to Machine Learning
Learning objectives:
•Understand (at a high level) the components of the machine learning pipeline and
their connections.
•Understand the main types of machine learning and differences between supervised
and unsupervised learning, as well as regression and classification.
•Understand and employ basic feature engineering – encoding categorical variables;
feature scaling and transformations, etc.
With ever increasing amounts of data, the need for automated methods of data anal-
ysis has grown.Machine learningaims to automatically detect patterns in data and
use these patterns to predict future data or outcomes of interests and make decisions.
2
Machine learning algorithms are now used ubiquitously, for example, to automate which
ads to display, make recommendations, detect anomalies, in natural language process-
ing and computer vision, in self-driving cars, for automated diagnosis and personalized
medicine, and many more.
Historically, the term machine learning (ML) was coined by Arthur Samuel in 1959,
and it can be viewed as a subfield of Artificial Intelligence (AI). Artificial Intelligence
is a field of computer science that aims to make computers, ormachines, “smart” by
simulating human intelligence. Instead of explicitly programming this intelligence, ma-
chine learning is based on the idea that machines can learn intelligent behavior from
data. Thus, machine learning is closely tied to statistics but differs slightly in terms of
its emphasis and terminology. We will see throughout the course that ML algorithms
usually involve ‘training’ (estimation) of a model using a so-called training data set and,
in a second step, ‘testing’ of the model to understand its performance in future data.
In this course, we aim to teach some of the fundamentals of machine learning (we
certainly can’t cover everything!) that are both used in many practical applications and
form the basis of research. Our aim is not to view machine learning methods as a black
box. Indeed, no single approach will perform well in all applications. Instead, we aim
to understand:
•the assumptions and intuitions of the methods, as well as the trade-offs, advantages,
and limitations of each method in what can be achieved,
•how to implement the methods in practice with Python,
•how to select, compare and evaluate different methods.

2.1 Data Collection
Data collection is the process of gathering and measuring information from different
sources. Collecting data and designing sample surveys in theright wayis critical for
appropriate and sensible analysis. This component of the pipeline requires a deeper
understanding, that is out of scope for this course. Instead, we will focus on available
4
data sets, but emphasize the importance of understanding how the data was collected
and any limitations of the data.
In particular, you should make sure that your data comes from a reliable source and
is of good quality. Do not simply assume that because a data set has been used by other
papers that is of good quality. Remember “Garbage in, garbage out”; that is, if you
train your model on bad data, you will most likely generate a bad model.
2.2 Exploratory Data Analysis
Before applying any machine learning methods, anexploratory data analysis(EDA)
should be carried out to visualize the data and summarize the main characteristics.
This is important to check model assumptions, investigate transformations, and help to
identify outliers, patterns, and trends in the data.
EDA allows you to gain a better understanding of the data. This knowledge can
help you make better decisions about how to preprocess and transform the data before
modeling it and find any issues or problems that may need to be fixed. By performing
EDA, you can learn more about how your data is organized, which can help you choose
the right features and algorithms. Every ML model makes assumptions, and EDA is
crucial to help check the validity of these assumptions. During EDA, you might want
to explore the spread of the data, search for patterns and trends, and find any outliers
or oddities. It is also important to survey the literature at this phase, which can also
provide valuable information.
Additionally, EDA can help you check if the data is truly a representative sample
of the problem you are trying to solve. In general, any estimates, statements, and
decisions from your model are only applicable to the population represented by the
data. For example, a model for automating admissions to MSc programs trained on a
sample of students admitted to MSc programs in the School of Mathematics, University
of Edinburgh, most likely will not be applicable to the wider university, as the math
students may not be representative of all Edinburgh University students. A better
understanding of your data through EDA allows you to avoid overstating your findings
and making statements that are not supported by your data.
EDA can also help to uncover if the data is biased or skewed in a way that would affect
the model’s performance. For example, consider a model for automatically screening job
applicants trained on a database of past applicants. Suppose that in the past, the hiring
manager decided to never hire a female. The model will detect and propagate this bias
in the future. Through EDA, you can investigate if any biases are present (particularly,
5
with respect to sensitive variables, such as race and gender). While out of scope for
this course, the training algorithm can be altered to account for such biases (interested
students may refer to A Survery on Bias and Fairness in Machine Learning (2021)).
Lastly, it is important to be aware that different visualizations and summaries are
appropriate for different data types in EDA. For numerical data (e.g. data consists of
individuals’ income and height), standard summaries such as mean and standard devi-
ation can be used and appropriate visualizations include histograms and kernel density
estimates. To investigate relationships between pairs of numerical variables, a pair plot
(see example in Figure 2) and heat map of the correlations are useful. While the former
can be used to investigate relationships of general shape, the latter is more compact and
can be easier to interpret but focuses on linear or monotone relations (depending on the
type of correlation, which can e.g. be computed with pandas.Series.corr). Instead, cat-
egorical data (e.g. data consists of individuals’ gender and race) can be visualized with
count plots, and relationships between categorical and numerical variables (e.g. income
and gender) can be visualized with box plots, violin plots, swarm plots, or strip plots.
To test for independence between categorical variables, we can use a chi-square test of
independence. Every workshop will begin with EDA, where we see examples of different
visualizations and summaries for different data types.
2.2.1 Data Pre-Processing
The process of getting raw data ready for use in machine learning models is called
datapre-processing. The goal of data pre-processing is to make the data suitable for
modeling, by cleaning and transforming the data. Data pre-processing can include a
number of different steps, some of which we will see during the workshops, such as:
•Data cleaning.There are often a number of initial data cleaning steps required.
– Duplicatescan arise when items are accidentally entered in the database
twice and should be removed as they may bias our fitted model; however,
care should be taken to check they are not real data with identical values.
– Inconsistencies, such as typos and spelling mistakes, often occur in practice
and should be corrected. For example, consider a variable representing gender,
the different spellings{F, Female, FEMALE, female}entered by customers
all represent the category ‘female’. A naive approach that skips this check
would incorrectly treat these spellings as four different categories.
– Outliersare points that lie abnormally far from other observations and can
distort the model fit and results. In some cases, you may want to remove or
6
cap their values. However, care should be taken to check that they are really
outliers, as you may discard important information!
– Missing valuescannot be handled by most ML models. When available
you should consult with the data dictionary, literature, and any experts to
better understand the missingness mechanism and how best to cope with it.
