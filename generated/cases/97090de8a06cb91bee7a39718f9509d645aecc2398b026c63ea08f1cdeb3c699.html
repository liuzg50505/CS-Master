Abstract— Semantic analysis enables the conversion of raw
strings into representations that can be implied to have
meaning and can be reasoned over, compared, and aggregated.
In the domain of literatures, such as scholarly articles, books,
and citation networks, the applications of semantic analysis
also underlie topic modeling, metadata enrichment, citation
context, literature mapping and evidence synthesis. This paper
provides a survey of primary models and approaches to textual
semantic analysis in the context of text analytics in the realm of
literary and scientific literatures, including vector space
weighting, latent semantic analysis, models for topic modeling,
explicit semantic analysis, neural embeddings for words and
documents, as well as Transformer-based language models.
This includes bibliometric metrics of similarity — co-citation
and bibliographic coupling — that offer quotes similar or by
respect to the semantics of the literature landscape. Thus, in
addition to the peer-reviewed and canonical articles discussed,
this paper synthesizes how these approaches and models claim
to derive meaning from documents (literature), evaluates that
meaning for scholarly text, and critiques their related
assumptions, data requirements, and evaluation practice. This
paper incorporates lessons learned from models based in
traditional linear algebra as well as modern deep architecture
models, while also highlighting the inherent trade-offs
regarding transparency of meaning extraction from data, the
data-hungry requirements of certain models, and the
assumption of domain knowledge in their applications. The
conclusion offers method selection and hybridization advice,
focusing on explainable pipelines that combine text-based
semantics and citation signals that produce robust literary
intelligence (analytic) systems in the real world [1]-[10].
Keywords— Semantic analysis , document embeddings,
LDA, Word2Vec, BERT
I. OVERVIEW
Text analytics in the context of literatures centers on
identifying and organizing meaning from scholarly texts,
metadata, and citation relationships to carry out search and
retrieval, classify documents and sections, expand queries,
cluster the research fronts together, identify trends, and
summarize in a literatures context. In contrast to regular web
texts, scholarly papers, and corpuses, have a significant
density of domain-specific terminology, have an
organizational formal rhetorical structure, and have
significant relations with other documents by being encoded
in the form of references and citations. Therefore, when it
comes to semantic analyses, the analysis must analyze both
linguistic meaning but also literature-specific signals.
The classical pipeline starts with tokenization and
normalization of texts before features are constructed. Vector
space models with term-weighting schemes - e.g., TF, TF-
IDF, and BM25 use term importance based on text frequency
statistics within the entire corpus, which provided the basis
for retrieval and classification of documents historically[2].
These features are generally bag-of-words features that are
iterative to compute and have proven to be robust to noise
and fast, and effective in-text search matching. However,
bag-of-word features such as TF-IDF treat all terms in
isolation without consideration of the other terms in the
documents as they sacrifice order of terms, and synonyms,
while corresponding polysemous terms independently. Thus,
bag-of-words terms cannot represent deeper semantic
meaning - e.g., paraphrase equivalency, topical similarity
using a different vocabulary, or the rhetorical function of
sentences located in a paper.
Latent variable methods project texts into lower-
dimensional concept spaces in order to tackle
representational limitations. In Latent Semantic Analysis
(LSA), singular value decomposition is applied to the term‐
document matrix to identify latent concepts derived from co-
occurrence, while reducing noise [1]. LSA effectively
captures synonymy by clustering together terms that co-
occur in similar contexts, thus improving recall in response
to semantic queries. In topic modeling— specifically Latent
Dirichlet Allocation (LDA)— documents are assumed to be
generated from mixtures of latent topics, where topics are
distributions over words. This probabilistic framework
produces interpretable thematic decompositions, while
connecting topics to generative assumptions [3]. LSA and
LDA both improve access to semantic coherence at the
corpus-level, but both are sensitive to the vocabulary design,
stopword removal, and the hyperparameters for
dimensionality and topic counts.
Explicit Semantic Analysis (ESA) extends latent methods
by using an external encyclopedia (i.e., Wikipedia) to embed
text in a high-dimensional concept space made up of article
contents, which allows semantic relatedness computations
that utilize the structure of an encyclopedic source [4]. ESA
semantically relates the meaning of words and documents to
concepts that humans can understand. ESA has been applied
successfully for cross-domain relatedness and query
expansion, which is useful in literature discovery when there
is domain drift (i.e., when the keyword context doesn't map
to findings).
Distributed representations revolutionized the paradigm
by employing predictive objectives to apply learning to
dense vectors that represent meaning. Word2Vec models
learn word embeddings using local context prediction,
allowing vector arithmetic to represent syntactic and
semantic regularities [5]. Paragraph Vector (Doc2Vec)
extends word representations to sentence and document
representations through predicting word given a learned
document representation, often called a distributed vector [8].
These embeddings develop improved clustering,
classification and recommendation for academic corpora,
where documents have unique vocabularies between
subfields.
While learning distributed embeddings, Transformers
utilize attention mechanisms to impose long-range
dependencies without recurrence, generating deep contextual
representations in which a token's meaning depends on input
that may comprise the entire sequence [6]. BERT and other
pretrained language models learn bidirectional contextual
embeddings using masked language modeling and next
sentence prediction. Furthermore, they can be fine-tuned to
accomplish specific tasks such as sentence classification,
passage retrieval, and citation intent detection [7]. These
contemporary models for literature analytics help with
assigned denotations or meaning. Not only do they better
model polysemy, but they also do better to handle rhetorical
structure, resulting in higher empirical understanding of
abstracts, methodology, and conclusions.
Alongside text-based semantics, bibliometric approaches
allow researchers to determine meaning conveyed by citation
practices. Co-citation analysis counts the number of times a
pair of documents are cited together, indicating the
documents' intellectual proximity as determined by the
community [9]. Bibliographic coupling counts the number of
documents in a reference list that overlap, modeling
similarity based on the reference lists of separate documents,
thus indicating that the documents pull on common previous
work [10]. These methods can map research fronts and
specialty structures to provide a visual representation of
semantics that emerge from scholarly practice, rather than
from text itself.

analytics in the academic arena are found throughout the
literature:
Term-weighting is still paramount. Schemes such as
TF–IDF, BM25, and similar approaches to term-weighting
can establish effective baseline systems for retrieval and
classification tasks across large corpora because they are
relatively simple and robust [2]. When these methods are
used in indexing systems there are limitations, however they
are often comparable systems in situations where an exact
lexical match is desired.
